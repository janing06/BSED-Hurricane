What is Hurricane?



Hurricanes are mighty and violent storms, capable of producing winds reaching speeds of 119 kilometers per hour (74 miles per hour), which surpasses the swiftness of a cheetah, the fastest land animal. These hurricane winds can potentially cause destruction, affecting everything from vegetation to man-made structures.

Hurricanes originate from the heat of warm ocean waters, and they occasionally make landfall. Upon reaching land, hurricanes generate a force that propels a substantial mass of seawater toward the shore, known as a storm surge. The combined impact of torrential rainfall and the storm surge can lead to flooding caused by a hurricane.

When hurricanes make landfall, they have the potential to inflict extensive devastation, primarily attributed to flooding and storm surges. Storm surge transpires when the ocean level rises along the coastline due to the storm's force. Additionally, hurricanes wreak havoc by uprooting trees and causing damage to homes with their powerful winds. It is not uncommon for numerous minor tornadoes to accompany many hurricanes.

Once hurricane forms, meteorologists forecast its trajectory and gauge its intensity. These predictions serve as crucial information for individuals to prepare for the impending storm.



What Are the Parts of Hurricane?


Eye

The eye is the "hole" at the center of the storm. Winds are light in this area. Skies are partly cloudy, and sometimes even clear.

Eye wall

The eye wall is a ring of thunderstorms. These storms swirl around the eye. The wall is where winds are strongest, and rain is heaviest.

Rain bands

Bands of clouds and rain go far out from a hurricane's eye wall. These bands stretch for hundreds of miles. They contain thunderstorms and sometimes tornadoes.

Categories of Hurricanes

Hurricanes can be classified into five distinct types or categories, with their classification determined by the Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale, which measures their intensity based on wind speed.


Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale


The Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale is a 1 to 5 rating based on a hurricane's sustained wind speed. This scale estimates potential property damage. Hurricanes reaching Category 3 and higher are considered major hurricanes because of their potential for significant loss of life and damage. Category 1 and 2 storms are still dangerous, however, and require preventative measures. In the western North Pacific, the term "super typhoon" is used for tropical cyclones with sustained winds exceeding 150 mph. Note that all winds are using the U.S. 1-minute average.


Category One Hurricane


Winds 74-95 mph (64-82 kt or 119-153 km/hr). Very dangerous winds will produce some damage: Well-constructed frame homes could have damage to roof, shingles, vinyl siding and gutters. Large branches of trees will snap and shallowly rooted trees may be toppled. Extensive damage to power lines and poles likely will result in power outages that could last a few to several days. Irene of 1999, Katrina of 2005, and several others were Category One hurricanes at landfall in South Florida.

Category Two Hurricane


Winds 96-110 mph (83-95 kt or 154-177 km/hr). Extremely dangerous winds will cause extensive damage: Well-constructed frame homes could sustain major roof and siding damage. Many shallowly rooted trees will be snapped or uprooted and block numerous roads. Near-total power loss is expected with outages that could last from several days to weeks. Frances of 2004 was a Category Two when it hit just north of Palm Beach County, along with at least 10 other hurricanes which have struck South Florida since 1894.

Category Three Hurricane


Winds 111-129 mph (96-112 kt or 178-208 km/hr). Devastating damage will occur: Well-built framed homes may incur major damage or removal of roof decking and gable ends. Many trees will be snapped or uprooted, blocking numerous roads. Electricity and water will be unavailable for several days to weeks after the storm passes. Unnamed hurricanes of 1909, 1910, 1929, 1933, 1945, and 1949 were all Category 3 storms when they struck South Florida, as were King of 1950, Betsy of 1965, Jeanne of 2004, and Irma of 2017.

Category Four Hurricane


Winds 130-156 mph (113-136 kt or 209-251 km/hr). Catastrophic damage will occur: Well-built framed homes can sustain severe damage with loss of most of the roof structure and/or some exterior walls. Most trees will be snapped or uprooted and power poles downed. Fallen trees and power poles will isolate residential areas. Power outages will last weeks to possibly months. Most of the area will be uninhabitable for weeks or months. The 1888, 1900, 1919, 1926 Great Miami, 1928 Lake Okeechobee/Palm Beach, 1947, Donna of 1960 made landfall in South Florida as Category Four hurricanes.

Category Five Hurricane


Winds 157 mph or higher (137 kt or higher or 252 km/hr or higher). Catastrophic damage will occur: A high percentage of framed homes will be destroyed, with total roof failure and wall collapse. Fallen trees and power poles will isolate residential areas. Power outages will last for weeks to possibly months. Most of the area will be uninhabitable for weeks or months. The Keys Hurricane of 1935 and Andrew of 1992 made landfall in South Florida as Category Five hurricanes.

Causes of Hurricanes

How are hurricanes formed?


Hurricanes arise due to the convergence of several variables, including warm ocean, humid, warm air, and weakened upper-level winds. The process begins when enormous amounts of damp and warm moisture from the ocean surface swiftly ascend and interact with other substantial air masses. This collision prompts the condensation of warm water vapor, leading to the development of storm clouds and rainfall. Because of this condensation process, latent heat is released, which warms the cooler air above. This warming process causes colder air to rise, making way for warmer, damper air to rise from deeper portions of the ocean.


As the storm intensifies, it draws in a greater volume of warm, moisture-rich air, leading to a notable exchange of heat from the ocean's surface to the encompassing atmosphere. This ongoing heat exchange generates a wind pattern that revolves around a comparatively tranquil center, resembling the swirling motion of water going down a drain.

Conditions for Hurricane Development


Temperature

For hurricanes to develop or maintain their strength, it is necessary for ocean waters to have a temperature higher than 26 degrees Celsius (79 degrees Fahrenheit). If these storms encounter water temperatures below this threshold, they either fail to form or rapidly lose their intensity. Typically, the tropical East Pacific and tropical Atlantic Ocean regions experience temperatures that surpass this critical limit.

Coriolis Force

Hurricanes form due to the Earth's spin and the resulting Coriolis force, which is essential to their development. Without these elements, hurricanes would not be able to develop. Furthermore, hurricanes cannot emerge within 5 degrees latitude of the equator due to the differing intensity of the Coriolis force. This force is most powerful at the poles and least powerful towards the equator. In the Northern Hemisphere, the Coriolis force causes low-pressure systems to spin counterclockwise, but in the Southern Hemisphere, it causes low-pressure systems to spin clockwise.

Saturated lapse rate

The most significant latent heat will be released if the lapse rate becomes saturated. Hurricanes, which are classified as warm core storms, produce heat through the convective rising of water vapor that condenses around the eye wall. To sustain the upward movement and condensation of water vapor within ascending air parcels, the lapse rate surrounding the eyewall needs to be unstable.

Low vertical wind shear

Wind shear means a change in wind speed as you move higher in the atmosphere. A significant difference in wind speed between the lower and upper levels can disrupt the structure of a storm. This disruption occurs because the high-speed winds at higher altitudes displace the warm air above the storm's center and limit the vertical movement of air within the storm. As a result, if the upper-level winds are powerful, hurricanes or other severe storms may fail to form.

Humidity

The presence of dry air in the middle levels of the atmosphere has two impacts that hinder storm formation. Initially, the dry air causes liquid water to evaporate, which cools the surrounding surroundings. This cooling process weakens the hurricane's heated core structure and inhibits the upward expansion of convection inside it. Second, the dry air in the mid-levels can cause a trade wind inversion, which is comparable to descending air in a high-pressure system. This inversion forms a layer in the atmosphere's mid-levels characterized by high temperatures and a lack of moisture. This phenomenon happens because of middle level air sinking and adiabatic warming.

Tropical wave

Thunderstorm complexes that move away from the African coast in the Atlantic Ocean often evolve into hurricanes. As they progress, they transform into a mid-tropospheric wave. If these waves encounter conducive conditions, including those mentioned earlier, they will intensify and develop into tropical storms or hurricanes. In the Eastern Pacific, hurricanes can originate from a monsoonal trough or a mid-tropospheric wave.

Hurricane Impacts

Impacts of Hurricanes



STORM SURGE

A storm surge is a disproportionately large rise in water levels caused by a storm that exceeds the projected tidal levels. It is not to be confused with a storm tide caused by the combined influence of the storm surge and the astronomical tide. The substantial fluctuation in water level can lead to extensive flooding in coastal areas, particularly when the storm surge aligns with the normal high tide. This combination can result in storm tides surpassing 20 feet or even higher in certain cases. The magnitude of a hurricane's storm surge is influenced by factors such as the hurricane's movement, speed, and strength.



Inland Flooding

In the past few decades, inland freshwater flooding has emerged as the most dangerous threat caused by tropical cyclones. From 1970 to 1999, approximately 60% of flood-related deaths attributed to tropical cyclones occurred in areas inland from where the storm initially made landfall—within this 60%, nearly a quarter (23%) of fatalities occurred when individuals drowned inside their vehicles or while trying to evacuate from them.



Strong winds

The powerful winds of a hurricane can cause severe destruction, including demolishing buildings and mobile homes, toppling trees and power lines, and transforming objects like signs and roofs into dangerous projectiles. These winds reach their highest intensity near the storm's center. As the storm moves towards inland areas, the wind speeds decrease rapidly, but hurricane-force winds can still be experienced up to 150 miles away from the coast. The strength and speed of the storm are directly proportional to the extent of inland areas affected by these intense hurricane-force winds.



Tornadoes

Tornadoes result from tropical storms making landfall, contributing to the already destructive power of the storm. The highest likelihood of tornado formation occurs in the right front quadrant of a hurricane, specifically in rainbands far from the central core. However, tornadoes can also occur near the eyewall of the storm. Compared to supercell thunderstorms, tornadoes associated with tropical systems are generally less intense. Nevertheless, when these tornadoes combine with the broader area of hurricane-force winds, they can still cause considerable damage and potentially threaten life.

Introduction to Hurricane



"Those who cannot remember the past are condemned to repeat it," wrote philosopher and essayist George Santayana. This saying about hurricanes can be seen as both accurate and inaccurate, depending on one's viewpoint. It is incorrect because regardless of our knowledge about past hurricanes, new ones will emerge, leading to a continuous cycle of extensive destruction, economic hardship, and loss of life. However, by acknowledging and comprehending the historical patterns of hurricanes, we can make informed decisions on how to effectively handle the ongoing onslaught of these potent storms, ultimately reducing their impact.

Hurricanes are formidable and devastating natural events that rank among the most potent phenomena on Earth. These storms can inflict substantial harm upon residences, structures, and infrastructure, radically leading, tragically leading to loss of life and the displacement of entire communities. It is essential to adequately comprehend the factors contributing to hurricanes and their repercussions to sufficiently prepare for and minimize their destructive impact.

In this brochure, we will explore the factors that contribute to the formation and intensification of hurricanes, as well as the ways in which they can impact both coastal and inland regions. Whether you live in a hurricane-prone area or are simply interested in learning more about these devastating storms, we hope that this brochure will provide valuable insights into the impact and science of hurricanes.



History of Hurricane

In 1274, as the legend goes, Khublai Khan dispatched his army to conquer Japan. However, their plans were thwarted when a sudden and formidable typhoon, a tropical cyclone originating in the Pacific, unexpectedly struck. This devastating natural disaster destroyed Khan's fleet, ultimately leading to the salvation of Japan.









The Mongols attempted to invade Japan with a larger army after a few years had passed. However, another typhoon materialized again and effectively transformed the invading fleet into scattered pieces of driftwood.



The Japanese believed that their emperor called the storms to protect them from the invading forces, and they referred to these typhoons as the "Divine winds" or "KAMIKAZE."



The indigenous cultures of the Caribbean and Central America, such as the Taino and the Maya, had a rich tradition of storytelling that included tales of Hurucan, a deity associated with wind and storms. Remarkably, their ancient artifacts prove that these cultures had already recognized the spiral shape of storms, a concept that eluded Western scientists until 1831.



The picture on the left depicts the Taino representation of the huraca'n, which was placed on top of Hurricane Katrina as it approached New Orleans, Louisiana, after leaving the Caribbean. The Taino people, the indigenous inhabitants of the Caribbean, deeply understood the annual weather disturbances that disrupted their idyllic environment. The huraca'n was seen as a powerful female deity known as the "Rider of the Winds." Female ceramists depicted her on ceramics as a head with "S" shaped arms extending from her shoulders or temples, resembling the flailing wings of a bird. One notable difference between the Taino perspective and that of modern meteorologists was that the Taino regarded the hurricane's "eye" as a woman's face.


It didn't go by the names Wilma, Katrina, Ivan, Camille, Diane, Rupert, or Ann. Long before the birth of Christ, she was called Guabancex (pronounced gwa-ban-seh), the vengeful female spirit of the hurricane. This powerful goddess was filled with rage and accompanied by two male partners. Together, they unleashed destruction upon the lands of the Caribbean, Central America, and North America.



“According to an early Spanish chronicler in the Caribbean, it is said that when Guabancex is enraged, she causes the oceans to surge, resulting in the movement of winds and water that can throw houses to the ground and uproot trees.”


Tropical cyclones are referred to by various names depending on the ocean they occur in. However, Atlantic Hurricanes like Katrina and Sandy have gained widespread recognition among these cyclones and are considered highly prominent. These storms are notorious for their extreme intensity and destructive nature, making them among the most severe storms ever observed in the Atlantic region.
